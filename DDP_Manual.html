HOW THE SYSTEM WORKS
XP, stages, levels. It's a game because games work.

Your brain is a reward machine. The same circuit that traps you in TikTok is the one we will use to pull you out. Every habit gives XP. XP unlocks stages. Stages unlock new habits. Levels go up forever with no cap. The progression is the point.

This isn't a gimmick. This is the same psychology your phone uses against you, redirected at something that actually serves you.

S AND P, EXPLAINED

S stands for Stage. S1, S2, S3, S4, S5, S6 are the six stages of the protocol. Habit IDs like S1.01 mean Stage 1, habit 1. Each stage builds on the previous one.

P stands for Priority. Every habit inside a stage has a priority level. P1 habits are non-negotiable, the foundation everything else stands on. P2 habits are important and build on those. P3 habits compound the gains. P4 habits are advanced additions for later stages. Always do P1 first. Always.

XP, THE CURRENCY

Every habit you complete adds XP to your total. XP only goes up. It never resets. A bad day doesn't subtract anything. You simply don't add. This is intentional. XP is the resilience layer: it reflects cumulative effort across the entire journey and never punishes a missed day. You never restart. You just keep going from where you stopped.

The tracker also tracks a daily streak. The streak is the challenge layer: it rewards consistency and pushes you to show up every day. To protect the streak, you earn Insurance Tokens by completing 7 consecutive perfect days. Tokens bank automatically, up to 5 maximum, and deploy automatically when you miss a day. If you've no tokens banked, the streak resets. The streak measures your edge. XP measures your foundation. Both matter, for different reasons.

XP values follow priority. P1 habits give the most XP because they have the most impact. Don't skip P1 to chase easier P2 or P3 habits. That's optimizing for the wrong number.

STAGES, THE PROGRESSION

There are six stages. Each one builds on the last. You don't pick which stage to do, you earn the next one with cumulative XP. When you unlock a new stage, the old habits don't disappear. They stack. By Stage 5, you're running 12 or more habits a day. This sounds like a lot until you realize most of them are automatic by then. The morning walk you struggled with in week 1 is just "what I do" by week 6.

Each stage has two unlock conditions that both must be met: the XP threshold and a minimum number of active days. You can't skip the time requirement by grinding XP on less important habits. The days are there because habits need repetition to wire in, and no amount of XP changes the biology. Both conditions unlock together, or neither does.

The Day Lock Rule
An active day is any day you complete at least one P1 habit. Days with only P2 or P3 habits don't count toward the day requirement. The minimums are 10, 14, 21, 30, and 41 days per stage. XP shows your effort. Days show your consistency. Both matter.

LEVELS, THE LADDER

Independent of stages, you've a level title. The level reflects your total cumulative XP. After level 10, every additional 5,000 XP is a new level. There's no end state. Sovereignty is a practice, not a destination.

STREAK INSURANCE TOKENS

Tokens are the only safety net in the system. You earn one token every 7 consecutive perfect days, meaning every habit checked for 7 days straight. You can bank up to 5 tokens. Tokens must be earned through discipline. They can't be manipulated or gamed. They're not a reward for showing up once. They're a reward for not breaking the chain for seven days. The rules of engagement. Read these every week. They're the spine.

These aren't suggestions. They're the operating principles that make every habit in this manual actually work. Skip the rules and you'll do the habits and wonder why they don't stick. The rules are the difference between a 30-day attempt and a permanent install.

Write, don't type.
Every reflection in this protocol, journal, weekly recap, dopamine audit, is done with pen and paper. Never on the phone. Never on a laptop. The friction is the feature. XP is cumulative. There's no streak to break. The real damage is never the bad day. The damage is the spiral after. The voice that says "well I already messed up, might as well scroll for 5 hours and start fresh Monday." That voice is the addiction protecting itself. People don't quit because they failed once. They quit because they decided one failure meant they ruined everything. A diluted protocol still works. A quit one doesn't. If you fall off, the recovery isn't restarting. THE TRAPS
Nine failure modes from inside the fight. Recognize them before they recognize you.

These are the traps I fell into. Repeatedly. I'm naming them so you don't have to discover them the hard way. When you feel one of these starting to pull at you, you'll have a label for it, and the label itself weakens the trap.

Read these. Recognize them. The recognition is half the defense.

TRAP 01
The Weak State
You don't fall for the phone when you're sharp. You fall when you're tired, slept badly, just got bad news, came home wrecked from work. The addiction wins by default in low-energy states. The protocol must be hardest to break exactly when you're weakest. That's why airplane mode at night is P1. It's the rule that protects you from yourself when you can't protect yourself.
TRAP 02
Proximity Equals Relapse
Phone in your pocket, on the bedside, on the desk within reach, guaranteed cheat. Willpower doesn't survive proximity. Airplane mode is good. Phone in another room is better. Phone in another room with notifications off is the goal. Make the wrong choice physically harder than the right one. Distance is architecture, not willpower.
TRAP 03
Notifications Are Poison
Even one ping creates the loop. "What if it's important?" Sounds, vibrations, banners, same circuit firing. Your nervous system reacts to the alert before your conscious mind even has a chance to evaluate it. By the time you're "deciding" to check, your brain is already mid-dose. Kill them all at the system level. The people who actually need to reach you'll call.
TRAP 04
Fake Rest
Lying down with the phone is not rest. You think you're recovering. You're getting hijacked while horizontal. Your nervous system isn't winding down, it's getting flooded with input your brain has to process. You stand up "rested" and feel more tired, more agitated, more empty. Real rest is silence, breath, sleep, a walk, a stare out the window.
TRAP 05
Loneliness and Boredom
These are the two biggest triggers. They're not problems. They're signals. The phone numbs them. Sitting with them is the work. Loneliness is your body telling you to connect with real people. Boredom is your brain telling you it has unused capacity. That's where ideas come from, where you come from. Numb the signal, miss the message.
TRAP 06
Unconscious Scrolling
The black hole. You don't notice you're in it. 30 minutes vanish. Sometimes an hour. You "wake up" mid-feed with no memory of how you got there. This is the addiction at full power. Self-awareness while scrolling is the first crack in the wall. The second you catch yourself, you've already won partial freedom. Catching yourself in the act is the most important skill in this entire protocol.
TRAP 07
The Public Phone
In public, alone at a cafe, waiting in line, the phone is armor. Armor against being seen as alone or doing nothing. You think people are judging you. They're not, they're on their phones. Reclaiming the right to just exist in public, phoneless and present, is a quiet revolution. The first time you sit at a cafe and just drink your coffee and watch the street, you'll feel something strange. That's your nervous system remembering what it's like to just be somewhere.
TRAP 08
Do Not Hate the Phone
Hate is dependency in reverse. You're still emotionally attached to the object, just the opposite direction. People who hate their phones are still controlled by their phones. The phone is a tool. A genuinely powerful one. The work isn't to destroy the relationship. The work is to redefine it. From compulsion to choice. From slot machine to instrument.
TRAP 09
The Substitution
You quit Instagram and fill the silence with podcasts. You delete TikTok and replace it with YouTube. You stop scrolling in bed and start watching Netflix in bed instead. The content changes. The mechanism doesn't. The brain gets the same hit from the same circuit regardless of how respectable the label on the input is. "I'm listening to a productivity podcast" is still your brain choosing input over presence. The substitution trap is the most convincing because it genuinely feels like progress. It's not. The real test is simple: can you sit with nothing? Silence, no input, just you and whatever is already in your head. If that's still intolerable, the work isn't done.

My phone sleeps in a different room. Not airplane mode on the nightstand, a different room entirely. I know myself. 3am, can't sleep, phone within reach, it's 4:30am when I put it down and tomorrow is wrecked. I stopped fighting that and just moved it out. Your setup matters more than your willpower. Stop trusting willpower, start changing rooms.

Also this: when I'm building something, the phone barely exists. When I'm empty and avoiding, the phone is everything. Use it to produce, not to consume. But you need to get enough distance first to even see which one you're doing.

The objective is removal, not addition. Most protocols pile on new habits before fixing the broken ones. We go the other way. Before you meditate, walk, read, or anything else, your night needs to stop being a slot machine and your morning needs to stop starting with a notification flood.

P1 / Foundation S1.01 +25 XP
Airplane Mode 1h Before Sleep
Phone goes on airplane mode one hour before bed. Stays there until morning. No exceptions.
+

Why This Is P1

The hour before sleep determines the next morning. If you scroll at 11pm, you wake up reaching for the phone at 7am. Late-night blue light blocks your body from producing melatonin, the hormone that makes you sleepy. The variable rewards of social media spike stress hormones when they should be falling. Your brain consolidates whatever you fed it last. Scroll, and you wake up anxious. Read, and you wake up calm.

Airplane mode is a physical commitment. Pressing that button signals to your brain: the day is over. Notifications cut. Messages can't reach you. Your nervous system gets permission to wind down for the first time in maybe years.

What This Actually Looks Like

✈️
The mechanic: 60 minutes before your target sleep time, swipe down, tap airplane mode. Phone stays on for alarms but is disconnected. Wi-Fi off too if you want to be strict.
📍
Where the phone goes: Out of the room. Not on the bedside, not across the room. A different room. If it's within reach you'll cheat. The other room is the rule.
⏰
Use it as your alarm: Airplane mode doesn't disable alarms. Set the alarm before you turn it on, put it in the other room, done.
⚠️
Common mistake: Checking "one last thing" right before. That last check can pull you in for 40 minutes. Airplane mode comes before the last check, not after.

What To Expect

Days 1 to 3: Anxiety. Restlessness. The feeling that you're missing something. This is real withdrawal. Your brain is asking for its scheduled dose. Sit with it. It passes in 20 minutes.

Days 4 to 7: Better sleep. You fall asleep faster. You wake up less. First physical evidence the protocol is working.

Day 14 onward: You don't even think about it. Phone in airplane mode at 10pm becomes as automatic as brushing your teeth.

Start tonight: Pick a target sleep time. Set a daily reminder for 60 minutes before. When it goes off, phone on airplane mode, other room. That's the entire habit.

First night I put it in the other room I slept differently. Can't fully explain it, just differently. Next morning, 30 minutes passed before I even thought about it. Those 30 minutes felt like something I had physically taken back. Small, quiet, mine. That morning I understood for the first time that the phone had been setting the temperature of my days before I was even awake enough to choose. If you do one thing from all of this, do this one. Tonight.

P1 / Foundation S1.02 +25 XP
No Social First 30 Min After Waking
No scroll, no messages, no news. Phone face down. The first 30 minutes belong to you.
+

Why This Is P1

The first 30 minutes after waking is when your brain is most open to input. Stress hormones are rising, the decision-making part of your brain is coming online, and whatever you put in gets printed on the rest of the day. Scroll first thing and you hand the day's emotional template to a stranger's algorithm. Sit with your coffee, and you hand the day to yourself.

This isn't about productivity. It's about ownership. The first 30 minutes are the one window where your day is completely yours. Once you start consuming, the day belongs to whoever made the content.

What This Actually Looks Like

🌅
Wake up. Phone in another room. Don't open it. Don't peek. Don't "just check the time," use a clock or a watch.
🚿
The first 30 minutes can include: water, bathroom, shower, getting dressed, making coffee, looking out the window. None of these require a phone. Humans did all of them for thousands of years without one.
📵
What if I need to check something? You don't. Whatever feels urgent won't actually be urgent. The urgency is the addiction.
⚠️
Common mistake: "Just checking messages quickly." That's not a check, that's the whole feed for 25 minutes. The 30-minute rule is binary. Phone open or phone closed. There's no "quick."

What To Expect

Day 1: Painful. You'll reach for it 8 to 10 times. That's normal. Put it back down each time. Each "no" is a win for your nervous system.

Week 1: Mornings start feeling slower in a good way. Time expands. You notice things, light coming through the window, the taste of your coffee, your own thoughts.

Week 4 onward: You realize you used to start every day already on the defensive, reacting to messages and drama. Now you start on the front foot.

The "emergency" loophole: "What if there's an emergency?" 99% of the time there's not. If there is, the person will call, not text. Set your phone to allow calls through during do-not-disturb if that makes you feel safer.

P2 / Important S1.03 +15 XP
Social Notifications Off, Blue Light Mode On
All social app notifications off at system level. Night mode on. Both done in one sitting in Settings.
+

Why This Matters

Every notification is an interruption your brain processes whether you want it to or not. The alert reaction, your body orienting toward the ping, happens before you decide to look. By the time you're "deciding" to check, you've already lost focus and already got a small dopamine hit. Notifications aren't information. They're interruptions designed to feel like emergencies.

The trick most apps use: send a vague notification ("Someone reacted to your post"). Vague triggers curiosity. Curiosity opens the app. Open app, see the feed, scroll for 20 minutes. The notification was the bait. The feed was the trap.

What To Turn Off

📵
Kill at the OS level: Settings, Notifications, then each app, Allow Notifications: OFF. Do this for Instagram, TikTok, X, Facebook, YouTube, Reddit, LinkedIn, Snapchat, every social platform you have.
💬
What stays on: Calls, SMS from real contacts, calendar, alarm. WhatsApp or Telegram is a personal choice. If you've group chats that fire constantly, mute them.
📧
Email: If the notification makes you stop what you're doing to check, turn it off. If you only check email twice a day anyway, leave it on.

Blue Light Mode

🌙
While you're already in Settings: turn on Night Shift (iPhone: Settings, Display and Brightness, Night Shift, Scheduled, Sunset to Sunrise) or Night Mode (Android: Settings, Display, Night Mode). This reduces the blue light your screen puts out in the evening, which helps your body actually get sleepy at night. One-time setup, 30 seconds.
⚠️
Why it matters: Blue light from screens in the evening blocks melatonin production, the hormone that makes you fall asleep. You can be physically tired and still take an hour to fall asleep because of this. Night mode doesn't fix everything but it removes one obstacle.

Do this in one sitting, today: 10 minutes in Settings. Turn off all social notifications. Turn on Night Shift or Night Mode. Done. You'll not miss anything important and you'll get hours of focus back per week.

P2 / Important S1.04 +10 XP
Water Before Phone
500ml of water immediately on waking. Before anything else. Before the phone.
+

Why It Is In The Protocol

This isn't really about hydration, though you're dehydrated after 8 hours of not drinking. It's about the order of operations. The first action of your day is the most important behavioral primer. If the first thing you do is drink water, your brain registers: we take care of the body first. If the first thing you do is grab the phone, your brain registers: we feed the addiction first.

There's something else. The motion of reaching for the bottle and bringing it to your mouth uses the same hand movement as reaching for the phone. You're physically practicing the replacement reflex. Morning after morning, water first, and the grab becomes water, not screen. The body learns the right reach.

How To Set It Up

🥛
Bottle on the desk next to your bed: Fill 500ml the night before. It's the first thing you see when you wake up. Open eyes, drink water, bathroom, start the day.
🍋
Optional upgrade: Add a pinch of salt and a squeeze of lemon. Better hydration, a small metabolic boost. Not required, just water is fine.
⚠️
Common mistake: Drinking water with the phone in the other hand. The whole point is water comes before phone. Phone stays away until water and 30 minutes are done.

Stack it: S1.04 (water) with S1.02 (no phone 30 min). Same morning, both habits, +35 XP. By week 2 it's automatic and you've already won the morning.

P2 / Important S1.05 +20 XP
Meditation 5 Min, Morning
Breath focus only. Timer. Eyes closed. Before the phone.
+

Why It Is In The Protocol

Meditation in this protocol isn't a spiritual practice. It's a training session for the decision-making part of your brain, the part responsible for impulse control, the part the addiction has spent years weakening. Five minutes of sitting still and watching your breath is five minutes of practicing the exact skill you need every time you decide not to pick up the phone.

The mechanism: when you sit and try to focus on the breath, your mind wanders. Every time you notice it has wandered and bring it back, you're doing a rep. The rep is awareness. The same awareness that catches you mid-scroll. Meditation is the gym for the muscle that wins this entire fight.

How To Actually Do It

🪑
Setup: Sit on a chair, on the floor, on a cushion. Back straight enough to stay alert, not so straight you're tense. Eyes closed.
⏱️
The instruction: Set a timer for 5 minutes. Notice the breath at the nostrils, the cool air coming in, the warm air going out. That's it. You're not trying to clear your mind. Your mind will wander. When you notice it has wandered, gently come back to the breath. That noticing is the entire practice.
📵
The timer rule: Use a physical timer if possible. If using the phone, set it before you start, put it face down, don't touch it. The phone is for the timer only.
⚠️
Common mistake: Thinking you're "bad at it" because your mind wanders. Wandering is the whole point. The skill isn'ticing.

What To Expect

Week 1: 5 minutes feels like 30. Your mind is loud. Sit anyway. Boredom is the gym (Rule 7).

Weeks 2 to 3: Slightly easier. You start noticing thoughts you didn't know you had. The quiet between thoughts gets wider.

Week 4 onward: 5 minutes starts to feel short. Throughout the day, you start catching yourself faster before reacting, before scrolling, before losing focus. That gap is meditation paying back.

Stack it: Right after water (S1.04), before phone. The sequence becomes: wake up, drink water, 5 min sit, bathroom and coffee, then eventually phone. The whole opening of the day is sovereign before the world gets in.

Evening Journal, 2 Questions
Pen and paper. After the day. Before sleep. Two questions, written by hand.
+

Why It Is In The Protocol

You can't change patterns you can't see. The journal is the lens. Two questions a night, written by hand, build the awareness that the entire protocol depends on. Without it, you're doing habits but not learning anything. With it, every day becomes data: what triggered me, what I avoided, what I felt.

Pen and paper is non-negotiable (Rule 1). The friction of handwriting forces your brain to slow down enough for the actual answer to surface. Typed answers stay on the surface. Handwritten answers go deeper, because the lower speed means you can't skip past the truth.

The Two Questions

P2 / ImportantS5.04+20 XP
Cold Shower, Last 15 Seconds
End of normal shower, turn to cold for 15 seconds. Direct dopamine reset.
+

Why Cold

Cold exposure spikes dopamine significantly, and unlike the phone's spikes, this one is followed by a sustained lift, not a crash. It's the cleanest dopamine hit available. Free, fast, and it trains the same circuit you override every time you don't pick up your phone: discomfort tolerance.

The cold also resets your nervous system. After 15 seconds of cold, the body releases alertness hormones, you feel focused and calm. One cold burst replaces three coffees for that ready feeling, without the crash.

How To Do It

🚿
End of shower: Wash normally. When done, turn the dial all the way to cold. Stay 15 seconds. Get out.
🌡️
How cold: Whatever your tap produces. In winter that's cold enough. In summer it's milder but still works. "As cold as your tap goes" is the rule, not "ice bath."
🫁
Breathe: The first 5 seconds your body wants to gasp. Don't. Breathe slow through the nose. Controlling your breath under cold is training the exact same muscle that controls the urge to pick up the phone.
⚠️
Common mistake: Skipping it because "it is cold today." That's exactly the day the habit pays the most. The discomfort is the workout (Rule 7).

Health note: If you've a heart condition, talk to a doctor first. For everyone else, 15 seconds of cold water at the end of a shower is safe and well studied.

My first cold showers were exactly like my first evenings without the phone. That same specific discomfort of knowing the warm option is right there. Knowing you could just reach over and end it. The first few times I didn't last long. Then something shifted. The cold stopped being a punishment and became the thing that set the tone for everything that followed. Now it's non-negotiable. I use it instead of a second or third coffee on the days when I'm dragging. It is faster, cleaner, and the alertness it produces doesn't crash. If you're relying on caffeine to feel human, try replacing one of those coffees with 30 seconds of cold first. You might be surprised which one you reach for less.

Weekly Reflection
THE WEEKLY RECAP
One review per week. Pen and paper. Patterns invisible at the daily level become obvious here.

Daily journals catch moments. Weekly recaps catch patterns. Some things only become visible across 7 days: your trigger windows, the days you keep failing, the specific contexts where the addiction wins. Without a weekly review, you keep falling into the same hole because you can't see its shape.

HOW TO DO IT

📅
Same time, same place, every week. Sunday evening is classic. The ritual of "this is when I review" makes it easier to actually do. Make it a small ceremony: sit at your desk, open the notebook, 25 quiet minutes.
✍️
Pen and paper. Same rule as the journal. The tracker shows the questions. You write in the notebook. Never type the recap.
📖
Read the week's daily entries first. Skim the past 7 days of journal answers before writing the recap. This is where patterns surface. You'll see the same trigger named on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday and realize it's a real pattern, not a one-off.
⏱️
20 to 30 minutes. Don't rush. Don't drag. The questions deserve real thought. That's the whole point.

What to look for: Repeating triggers, same context, same emotion. Wins you can repeat. Things you've stopped wanting, the most powerful signal of all. Track the direction, not perfection. A week with three bad days but visible learning beats seven perfect days with no insight.

Manual Re-Read: +25 XP Per Week
Re-reading a section of this manual is a weekly XP action. Not skimming. Not a glance at the stage you're in. Pick one section, read it in full, and write the key points in your own words in the weekly recap. This isn't a bonus. It's the mechanism by which the protocol installs itself permanently. The same paragraph means something completely different at Stage 3 than it did at Stage 1. You're not the same person reading it.

THE THREE RECAP QUESTIONS

These go in your notebook every Sunday. Pen and paper. After you've skimmed the week's daily journal entries. Give each one real thought. Honest answers only.

→
What did you consistently avoid this week that hadn'thing to do with the phone? Not about scrolling. About what the phone was protecting you from. The task you kept skipping. The feeling you kept outrunning. The conversation you kept postponing. Name it. It will be the same answer for several weeks before you do something about it. That repetition is the most useful data this entire protocol produces.
→
This week, what did the person you're becoming do, and what did the old version still manage to sneak in? Not feelings. Concrete actions. One for each. The person you're building showed up when you did something specific. The old version showed up in a specific moment too. Name both. The gap between them is exactly where you're right now.
→
What's one specific situation next week where you already know the old version will try to show up, and what's your exact plan for that moment? Not a vague intention. A plan for a specific moment. Tuesday evening after a hard shift. Sunday afternoon when there's nothing scheduled. The commute home on Thursday. Name the moment and write the move. Pre-commitment beats willpower every single time.
Mission Cycle
WEEKLY BOSSES
Six bosses in fixed sequence. Unlock at Stage 5. Cycle forever.

The weekly bosses target patterns that the daily habits don't reach directly. Each one isolates a specific addiction mechanism: visual stimulation, identity around apps, audio dependence, full detox tolerance, morning autonomy, the deep audit of your own avoidance. Six weeks per cycle. Then it cycles again, hitting deeper each time because you know more about yourself.

Order isn't optional. The sequence is the curriculum. App Delete teaches a small commitment. Dopamine Audit teaches the biggest one. Don't skip ahead.

01
App Delete
Delete one app you use out of boredom for 7 days. Pick the one your thumb finds without your conscious choice. Delete it from the phone, not just hide, not just log out, delete. After 7 days you can reinstall if you want. Most people don't. The challenge teaches you that the app's grip was never that strong. Your habit of opening it was.
+100 XP
02
Grayscale Week
Set your phone to grayscale for 7 days. All apps in black and white. Color is a deliberate design choice to keep you scrolling. Every red notification dot, every saturated thumbnail, every Instagram color palette is engineered to trigger a reaction. Strip the color and the phone instantly becomes 60% less appealing. You'll notice you reach for it less. iOS: Settings, Accessibility, Display and Text Size, Color Filters, Grayscale. Android: Settings, Digital Wellbeing, Bedtime Mode, Grayscale.
+120 XP
03
Silence Week
7 days with no music with lyrics, no podcasts, no background audio in any situation you control. Driving alone: silence. Working at home: silence. Showering: silence. Walking: silence. This is the deepest level of input fasting. The amount of time you've spent never alone with your own thoughts will shock you. By day 4, ideas surface, feelings move, problems solve themselves. By day 7 you'll be reluctant to put audio back on for things that didn't need it.
+150 XP
04
Full Detox
72 hours with zero social media of any kind. No checking. No browsing. No exceptions. No "just to message someone," use SMS or WhatsApp for that. Three days. Most people haven't gone 72 hours without social since they were teenagers. The first 24 hours are restless. The second 24 hours are clear. The third 24 hours are the cleanest mental state you've had in years. You'll come out with a recalibrated baseline.
+180 XP
05
Morning Pages
7 days: write 1 full page by hand immediately on waking, before any screen. Stream of consciousness. No filter. No editing. Whatever shows up goes on the page. The point is volume, not quality. The morning brain has access to material the daytime brain doesn't. Things you didn't know you were carrying come out. By day 4 or 5 you'll be writing things that surprise you. By day 7 you'll have a document about your own current life that no one else could have written.
+150 XP
06
Dopamine Audit
Write by hand a complete list of everything you use to avoid discomfort. Phone, scrolling, food, alcohol, weed, porn, video games, shopping, news, productivity theater, conflict avoidance, gym as escape, work as escape, everything. Be brutally honest. Then eliminate the first item on the list for 30 days. Not the easiest one, the first one that came to mind. This is the hardest weekly challenge and the most transformative. The audit alone, even without elimination, changes how you see yourself. The 30 days of elimination changes who you are.
+200 XP

How the cycle works: Boss 1 in week 1, Boss 2 in week 2, all the way to Boss 6 in week 6. Then back to Boss 1 in week 7, and you'll find it hits differently the second time. By round 3 or 4 you'll be choosing harder versions of each boss yourself. The framework holds. You sharpen inside it.
